Research overview

From satellites to streets — disentangling the urban climate signal

My PhD research sits at the intersection of urban climate science, satellite remote sensing, and regional numerical modelling. The unifying question: how do cities reshape the atmosphere overhead — its rainfall, its heat, and its human thermal stress — and how confidently can current observing systems and models detect those changes?

Theme 01

Urban rainfall trends & retrieval uncertainty in GPM IMERG

The Global Precipitation Measurement (GPM) IMERG product is the leading satellite-based rainfall record at sub-daily, near-global resolution. Yet its use over cities remains underexplored: surface emissivity, complex land cover, and tall structures complicate microwave and IR retrievals, and the algorithm's calibration was not designed for urban-scale interpretation.

I am quantifying how IMERG-derived urban rainfall climatologies and trends compare with rain-gauge and radar observations, and identifying the retrieval contexts under which urban rainfall signals are most likely to be genuine versus algorithmic.

Theme 02

Convection-permitting WRF simulations over Greater Sydney

Convection-permitting (kilometre-scale) WRF simulations are used to test whether urban expansion in Greater Sydney has measurably altered rainfall and thermal environments. By contrasting present-day and historical land-cover configurations under identical large-scale forcing, the simulations isolate the urban contribution from natural variability.

The framework couples WRF-ARW with urban canopy parameterisations and data-driven Local Climate Zone (LCZ) descriptions of city morphology (Theme 03).

Theme 03

Multi-decadal Local Climate Zone datasets for Sydney

Urban-climate modelling needs land cover that reflects how a city actually evolved, not a single snapshot. I developed a scalable, multi-decadal Local Climate Zone (LCZ) classification approach for Greater Sydney, published in PLOS Climate (Sharma et al., 2025), enabling consistent representation of urban form across decades.

The dataset is designed as an input for WRF and other regional models, supporting attribution of urbanisation impacts on local climate.

Theme 04

Urban heat and human thermal exposure

Surface and atmospheric heat anomalies translate into health-relevant thermal stress through human-scale biometeorology. I integrate WRF outputs, LCZ-classified morphology, and remote sensing to map how urban expansion changes human thermal exposure across Sydney, and how this is co-modulated by atmospheric circulation.

Methods & tools

How the work is built

Numerical modelling

WRF-ARW configured for convection-permitting urban simulations, coupled with urban canopy parameterisations and customised LCZ-driven land-surface inputs.

Earth observation

GPM IMERG (V06/V07), Landsat and Sentinel-2 for LCZ classification, ERA5 / MERRA-2 reanalyses, and gauge networks for validation.

Software

Python (Xarray, NumPy, Cartopy, matplotlib), NetCDF/Zarr workflows, MATLAB, ArcGIS / QGIS, and shell/HPC scripting for batch simulations.

Reproducibility

Code and analysis notebooks for individual studies are released alongside publications when permitted. See Projects.
